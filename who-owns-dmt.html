Who Owns the Molecule?

DMT is ancient and public-domain — so no one can own it. Yet a wave of companies is racing to patent everything around it: formulations, salts, deuterated analogs, dose regimens, devices, and the right to use it against a named illness. Here is who is trying, how they are faring, and the older question underneath it all — whether a sacred molecule can be owned at all.

✦The paradox: you cannot patent DMT, so they patent everything around it

N,N-DMT is old, natural, and exhaustively documented — it cannot be owned. What can be owned is the periphery: a particular formulation, a salt or crystal form, a deuterated analog, a dosing regimen, a delivery device, or a claim to use the molecule to treat a specific condition. This is the single most misunderstood point in the whole debate. When you read that a company has 'patented DMT,' it has almost always patented one of these peripheral things — not the molecule, which stays in the public domain.

✦The prior-art war

Because the molecule is ancient, many early filings were broad — claiming, in effect, obvious or already-known practice. Two watchdogs push back. Porta Sophia, a psychedelic prior-art library, files third-party submissions so examiners see the public record before granting; its work helped get all pending US claims rejected on GH Research's 5-MeO-DMT-for-depression application, and challenged 21 of 22 claims on a DMT-vape device. Freedom to Operate mounts formal post-grant challenges. And Usona Institute takes the opposite tack entirely — it refuses to patent and publishes openly, deliberately seeding prior art to keep the field open. A telling detail: GH Research's US claims were rejected while its European counterpart granted broadly — the same idea, opposite outcomes, one border apart.

✦Who can own a sacred plant?

The moral anchor of the whole debate predates the biotech rush. In 1986 an American, Loren Miller, was granted a US plant patent (PP5,751) on an ayahuasca vine cultivar he called 'Da Vine.' In 1999 a coalition of Amazonian Indigenous peoples (COICA) and the Center for International Environmental Law challenged it as biopiracy — the appropriation of a plant sacred to hundreds of communities. The patent office first rejected it, then reinstated it on technical grounds; it stood until it expired in 2003. The case remains the reference point for every question the current DMT rush raises about traditional knowledge, consent, and benefit-sharing.

✦Not everyone is enclosing

Three correctives worth holding. Compass Pathways, the name most associated with 'psychedelic patents,' has no DMT program — its patents are synthetic psilocybin. Philip Morris is, by some counts, the single largest holder of patents that merely mention DMT — a tobacco-chemistry artifact, not a medicine play; 'discloses DMT' is not 'owns DMT medicine.' And patents are not purely villainous: they are also how the very expensive clinical trials that could bring these into supervised care get funded. The honest frame is a contested territory, not a heist.

Questions

Can you patent DMT?

Not the molecule itself — N,N-DMT is public domain. Companies patent the periphery: a formulation, salt, deuterated analog, dose regimen, delivery device, or a use-to-treat-a-condition claim. That is why 'they patented DMT' is almost always inaccurate as stated.

Who owns the DMT patents?

The largest DMT-specific estate is Cybin's (which absorbed Small Pharma) — dozens granted and well over a hundred pending, including deuterated-DMT and injectable-formulation patents. Others include atai, Algernon, Biomind, and — on the related 5-MeO-DMT side — Beckley Psytech, acquired by Eli Lilly in July 2026. Compass Pathways holds psilocybin patents, not DMT.

Is it possible to own ayahuasca?

This was tested directly: a 1986 US plant patent on an ayahuasca vine ('Da Vine') was challenged by Amazonian Indigenous groups as biopiracy. It was reinstated on technical grounds and expired in 2003. The molecule and the plant are public domain; specific cultivars, formulations, and uses are where ownership claims are made.

Does patenting mean the medicine works?

No. A granted patent is a legal claim of novelty, not proof of safety or efficacy — that is what clinical trials decide. Many of these are early-stage, and grants are regularly rejected, narrowed, or challenged after the fact.

The one thing to know

You cannot patent DMT. You can patent a formulation, salt, analog, dose, device, or use — the periphery, not the molecule.

Biggest DMT estate

Cybin (absorbed Small Pharma): dozens granted, 170+ pending.

The counter-model

Usona refuses to patent and publishes openly to keep the field free.
